Early Life:

  • Born on July 21st 1899, in Cicero (now Oak Park), Illinois
  • Raised by Grace and Clarence Hemingway in a conservative suburb in Chicago
  • Spent most of his childhood life in a cabin at Michigan, where his father taught how to hunt, fish and appreciate the outdoors
  • During high school, worked for the school newspaper, focusing primarily on sports
  • Commited suicide on July 2nd 1961 at Ketchum, Idaho

Military Experience:

  • Went overseas as a soldier in WWI, as an ambulance driver in the Italian army
  • He met a nurse named Agnes von Kurowsky, when he was injured
  • Agnes later on accepted Hemingway's proposal on marriage, but soon left hi for another man
  • This devastated Hemingway but it became an inspiration for some of his works such as A Very Short Story and A Farewell to Arms
  • Soon returned to US and took jobs at the Toronto Star

Life in Europe:

  • Hemingway shortly met Hadley Richardson in Chicago, the woman who would later become Hemingway's first of many wives
  • Hemingway and Richardson married and soon left the US and moved to Paris
  • Hemingway soon met Gertrude Stein, his soon to be mentor and the one who helped jump start Hemingway's career as a renowned author
  • He acquainted himself with many of the great writers and artists of his generation, such as F. Scott Fitzgerald, Ezra Pound, Pablo Picasso, and James Joyce.
  • Hemingway and Hadley eventually divorced, because of his affair with Pauline Pfeiffer, Hadley's close friend
  • Pauline would soon become Hemingway's second wife

No amount of analysis can convey the quality of 'The Sun Also Rises'. It is a truly gripping story, told in a lean, hard, athletic, narrative prose that puts more literary English to shame... This novel is unquestionably one of the events of an unusually rich year in literature.

To Have and Have Not (1937):

He has moved steadily towards mastery of his technique... Technique however is not enough to make a great writer... There is evidence of no mental growth whatever; there is no better understanding in life... Essentially, this new novel is an empty book. - J. Donald Adams

Modernism
